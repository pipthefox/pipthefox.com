The letter had come in June, but Margaret didn't open it until September. She already knew what it said. The Coast Guard had been sending the same letter to keepers up and down the coast for three years now. *Modernization. Remote monitoring. Your dedicated service.*

She set it on the kitchen table next to the salt shaker, where it yellowed alongside the grocery lists and tide tables she'd been meaning to throw away since Harold died.

---

The lighthouse at Burnt Cape was not famous. It did not appear on postcards or in coffee table books about New England. It sat on a thumb of rock that jutted into the Atlantic at an angle the cartographers had never quite agreed on, and it had been saving fishermen's lives without fanfare since 1887. The lens was a Fourth Order Fresnel, which meant it was important enough to keep lit but not important enough to be beautiful. Margaret had always liked that about it. Utility without vanity. A working light.

She had come to Burnt Cape in 1981, the year Reagan was shot and the first space shuttle went up and her marriage to Harold Ames was three months old. Harold was the keeper; Margaret was the keeper's wife, which in those days meant you were also the keeper but nobody paid you for it. She learned the lens. She learned the fog signal. She learned the particular way the wind came around the north side of the tower in January, how it could knock you off the catwalk if you weren't ready for it.

Harold died in 2009 — a stroke, mid-sentence, telling her the weather buoy was reading six-foot swells. She finished listening to the marine forecast, then called the ambulance. The Coast Guard let her stay on as keeper. By then it was mostly ceremonial. The light ran itself, more or less. But somebody had to be there when it didn't.

---

The automated system arrived on a Tuesday in October, packed in three wooden crates that the crew from Portland unloaded onto the dock like coffins. Margaret watched from the kitchen window as two young men in orange vests carried components up the path. They were polite. They called her *ma'am* and asked where they could set up.

"The watch room," she said. "Where else would you put it?"

The younger one — he couldn't have been more than twenty-five — explained the system to her as he worked. GPS synchronization. Automatic weather reporting. Remote diagnostics via satellite. The light would flash its characteristic — two seconds on, two seconds off, ten second pause — with greater precision than any human keeper could manage. It would detect bulb failures and switch to backup automatically. It would report its own status every fifteen minutes to a monitoring station in Boston where nobody would look at the reports unless something went wrong.

"It's really very reliable," he said.

"So was I," said Margaret.

He blushed. She hadn't meant it the way it sounded, or maybe she had. Hard to tell at seventy-one.

---

They gave her two weeks to move out. She spent the first week not packing. She spent it the way she'd spent every week for forty-five years: she rose at five, checked the light, logged the weather, walked the grounds. The automated system was already running in parallel, but Margaret kept her logbook open on the desk in the watch room, recording what she saw.

*Oct 14. Wind NNW 15-20 kts. Overcast, ceiling 800 ft. Visibility 3 nm, dropping. Seas moderate. Noted cormorant colony has moved from south rocks to the lee side of the breakwater. Third year running they've shifted — follow-up with Audubon.*

The automated system recorded wind speed, temperature, barometric pressure, humidity, and visibility. It did not record cormorants.

*Oct 15. Wind variable, light. Fog overnight, burned off by 0900. Visibility unrestricted by afternoon. Lobster boat PATIENCE out of Machiasport passed close aboard at 0715, appears to be dragging gear. Will mention to Carl at the co-op.*

The automated system recorded the fog. It did not know Carl.

*Oct 17. Gale warning. Wind NW 35 gusting 50. Seas 12-15 ft. Light operating normally. Checked tower for leaks — found drip at south window, third landing. Caulking needed. Put bucket under it.*

The automated system, had it been alone, would have reported the weather and the light's status. The south window would have dripped quietly until the water found the wiring.

---

On her second to last night, Margaret climbed the tower one hundred and twelve steps to the lantern room. The light turned its slow rotation, the Fresnel lens breaking the beam into fingers that swept the dark water. She had replaced every part of this mechanism at least once. She knew which bolt seized in cold weather, which bearing sang in high humidity, which pane of storm glass had a bubble in it that caught the light wrong on clear nights.

She sat in the wooden chair she'd carried up forty years ago when her knees started complaining about the stairs, and she watched the light go around.

Two on. Two off. Pause. Two on. Two off. Pause.

The automated system would flash the same pattern. The photocell would trigger at exactly the right ambient light level. The GPS clock would keep the timing precise to fractions of a second. It would be, in every measurable way, the same light.

She thought about the fishermen out there in the dark, the ones hauling traps off Burnt Cape in November, in weather that had no business being worked in. For forty-five years she had been the reason the light was on. Not the mechanism — the reason. Someone who chose, every night, to climb the stairs and make sure. Someone who listened to the weather and thought, *tonight matters more than most.* Someone who would go out in a gale to clear ice from the lantern glass because a light that can't be seen isn't a light at all.

Did it matter, she wondered, that no one would choose anymore? That the light would come on the way a refrigerator light comes on — automatically, indifferently, because a sensor said so?

She didn't know. That was the honest answer. The fishermen would be just as safe. Safer, probably. The system wouldn't get the flu. It wouldn't slip on the stairs. It wouldn't die mid-sentence on a Tuesday afternoon.

But it wouldn't put a bucket under the drip, either.

---

On her last morning, Margaret wrote one final entry in the logbook. She left it open on the desk next to the new monitoring terminal, though she knew no one would read it.

*Oct 28. Final entry. Light operating normally. Seas calm. Wind light and variable. Weather clearing from the west. The cormorants are back on the south rocks.*

She closed the door behind her and walked down the path to the dock, where her niece was waiting with the car. She did not look back at the tower, because she didn't need to. She'd looked at it enough for one lifetime, and she knew exactly what it looked like. She could see it whenever she wanted — the white tower against the grey sky, the glass catching whatever light there was, the rocks below it battered and permanent.

The light came on that evening at 5:47 PM, triggered by the photocell. It flashed its pattern into the dark. Two on, two off, pause. Two on, two off, pause.

It was, in every measurable way, the same light.
